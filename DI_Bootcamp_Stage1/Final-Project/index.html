1888
Shmuel Yosef Agnon is born in Buczacz, Polish Galicia, part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, on July 26th. His birth coincides with the 9th of Av, a significant date in the Jewish calendar, though officially recorded on July 26. He grows up in a devout Jewish family, with his father, Shalom Mordechai Halevy, working in the fur trade and his mother coming from a background tied to the Mitnagdim.

Early 1900s
Agnon begins writing in Hebrew and Yiddish at the age of eight, showing early signs of literary talent. By fifteen, he publishes his first poem, a Yiddish piece about the Kabbalist Joseph della Reina, marking the start of his prolific writing career. He continues to publish poems and stories in Hebrew and Yiddish, gaining recognition in the literary circles of Galicia.

1908
Agnon moves to Jaffa, Ottoman Palestine, seeking new inspirations and a connection to the land of Israel. He publishes "Agunot," a story that earns him considerable attention and marks his first significant work in the land. The pen name "Agnon" is adopted from this story, eventually becoming his official surname.

1913-1924
Agnon relocates to Germany, where he immerses himself in the literary scene and meets Esther Marx, whom he marries in 1920. In Germany, he finds a patron in Salman Schocken, who supports him financially, allowing Agnon to focus on his writing. A fire in 1924 destroys his manuscripts and rare book collection, a traumatic event that deeply impacts him and influences his future works.

1924
After the fire, Agnon returns to Palestine, settling in Jerusalem's Talpiot neighborhood with his family. His return marks a new chapter in his literary career, focusing on themes of Jewish life and history. The anti-Jewish riots of 1929 lead to the destruction of his library once again, reinforcing themes of loss and exile in his writings.

1931-1945
"Hachnasat Kalla" (The Bridal Canopy) is published in 1931, establishing Agnon as a central figure in Hebrew literature. He continues to write, publishing "Sippur Pashut" (A Simple Story) in 1935 and "Tmol Shilshom" (Only Yesterday) in 1945, both of which receive critical acclaim. His works during this period are celebrated for their exploration of Jewish life, tradition, and the complexities of modernity.

1966
Agnon is awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature, becoming the first Hebrew writer to receive the honor. The award recognizes his profound contributions to Jewish literature, blending traditional narratives with modern themes. This accolade cements his legacy as one of the most influential figures in Jewish literary history.

1970
Shmuel Yosef Agnon passes away on February 17th in Jerusalem, leaving behind a rich legacy of literary work that continues to be studied and revered. His death marks the end of an era in Hebrew literature, but his influence persists through his contributions to Jewish culture and literature. Agnon's life and works remain a beacon for writers and readers interested in the complexities of Jewish identity, history, and faith.
